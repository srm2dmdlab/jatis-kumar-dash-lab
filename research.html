What we work on

Research

Growth and characterization of 2D layered materials — dichalcogenides, oxides, and alloy films — pushed toward real device applications.

Growth · TMDs

Large-area 2D layered dichalcogenides for devices

We grow layered transition metal dichalcogenides — NbS₂, SnS₂, and MoS₂ — using physical vapor deposition (magnetron sputtering in high vacuum) and CVD growth in a programmable quartz-tube furnace, co-evaporating precursors to form ultrathin films. Structure and texture are studied with grazing-incidence XRD and RHEED pole-figure analysis, alongside XPS, Raman scattering, AFM, and UV-Vis spectroscopy. These films have optical bandgaps in the 1–3 eV range, putting them in reach of optoelectronic and photovoltaic applications. SnS in particular is earth-abundant and non-toxic, with a bandgap of 1.0–1.5 eV suited to solar absorbers, while SnS₂ (2.0–2.5 eV) shows fast, microsecond-scale photocurrent response — both phases can be grown on low-cost glass and quartz substrates, with bandgap and phase tunable through growth conditions. We're now extending this toward heterostructure devices, and have also worked on wide-bandgap oxide semiconductors such as Nb₂O₅ and MoO₃.

Growth · 2D Oxides

Hetero-epitaxial 2D transition metal oxides

2D transition metal oxides (TMOs) remain far less explored than graphene or TMDs, largely for lack of a good growth route to layered form — despite offering wide bandgaps, low carrier concentration, and high dielectric constants that suppress Coulomb scattering. We've shown that layered α-MoO₃, a chemically stable n-type semiconductor, can be grown by van der Waals epitaxy on other 2D substrates. Using a low-cost, modified hot-plate method, we produced highly oriented α-MoO₃ layers — from bilayers up to a few layers thick — on hexagonal boron nitride and graphene, with good interfacial texture and few defects. Conductive-AFM I–V measurements on the resulting MoO₃–hBN and MoO₃–graphene heterostructures point to real potential as tunneling barriers, dielectrics, and channel materials for high-power electronics — a step toward a broader family of 2D-oxide-based devices.

Alloy Films

Ge₁₋ₓSnₓ direct-bandgap alloys on glass

Ge₁₋ₓSnₓ alloys are of interest for silicon-compatible optoelectronics, since alloying germanium with a few percent tin can drive its indirect bandgap toward direct — and even a small shift in Sn content measurably tunes the optical gap. Where most prior work relied on single-crystal substrates and techniques like MBE, we developed a route to grow biaxial Ge₁₋ₓSnₓ(111) films directly on glass, at low temperature, via sequential physical vapor deposition: a biaxial CaF₂ buffer layer, then a heteroepitaxial Sn layer, followed by Ge grown at 200–350 °C. The predeposited Sn promotes Ge diffusion and crystallization and substitutes into the Ge lattice as it forms, with clean epitaxial relationships confirmed by X-ray pole figures. XRD, pole-figure analysis, TEM, EDX, and Raman spectroscopy together confirmed uniform Sn incorporation and tracked how texture develops with growth temperature — a cost-effective alternative to single-crystal-substrate growth for biaxial semiconductor films.

Foundational Work

Si-Ge nanostructures on high-index Si surfaces

Earlier doctoral work focused on molecular beam epitaxy growth of self-assembled Si₁₋ₓGeₓ nanostructures on high-index silicon surfaces — Si(5 5 12), Si(5 5 7), and Si(5 5 3) — whose stepped, anisotropic terraces make them useful templates for aligned 1D nanostructure growth. Across all three surfaces, Ge islands showed a shape evolution from nanoparticles to trapezoidal structures with increasing thickness, aligned along the step-edge direction, and a universal growth-dynamics scaling captured by a kinetic Monte Carlo model. STEM, high-resolution XRD, and Rutherford backscattering spectrometry confirmed Ge–Si intermixing quantitatively — groundwork that carried directly into the group's later 2D materials growth work.

Looking ahead

Future Research Directions

Heterostructures of 2D layered materials

Combining growth experience from MBE, oblique-angle PVD, CVD, and sputtering — used previously to fabricate NbS₂, MoS₂, and SnS₂ — toward contamination-free 2D TMDC heterostacks, with a focus on grain-boundary control, crystal orientation, and flexible/transparent device integration.

New 2D thermoelectric materials

With roughly two-thirds of used energy lost as waste heat, solid-state thermoelectric conversion via the Seebeck and Peltier effects is an appealing route to recover it. We're pursuing new 2D metal chalcogenide and oxide materials, and their heterostructures, for efficient thermoelectric devices.
